Musonius Multisig Vault

How Musonius works

A Bitcoin security solution. Sovereign by design. Built to outlast you.

Why this exists.

Self-custody matters to you. Not your keys, not your coins.

But self-custody alone is hard. A lost device. A forgotten location. A bad year. The setups that look like multi-key safety on paper are often a single point of failure in practice.

So you have looked at collaborative security. A professional alongside you. A multi-key arrangement designed to survive what solo cannot. It is a real improvement.

But it ends in the same place every time. Trust. Trust that the provider — even the most honest one, running rigorous procedures — will not collude. Trust that their policies hold under court orders, ownership changes, regulatory shifts, employee turnover. Trust that the company you signed up with is the same company in ten years.

Ask any of them two questions: What happens when I want to leave? What happens when you can’t help me? If the answer to either requires the provider’s active participation — they must co-sign your exit, or their infrastructure must be running for recovery to work — you do not have self-custody. You have custody at the company’s pleasure.

That is the gap Musonius is built to close.

How Musonius answers it.

Musonius is built around one rule: while you are able to act, every transaction in your vault requires your signature. No override. No “we’ll sign on your authorization.” Your hardware device, your physical presence, every time.

The rule holds because the math is built to enforce it. Musonius the platform holds no keys — it cannot sign for anything in your vault, ever. There is no platform key to compromise, no override to subvert, no employee discipline that needs to hold. This is not policy — it is the structural absence of the thing that would make policy necessary.

The vault’s rules, written into the Bitcoin blockchain when the vault is created, require your key for the everyday path and for the short-cooldown recovery path. Two of your other keyholders cannot move funds while you are present and able to act. This is not a promise — it is the script.

The rule holds until you cannot act. If you lose your key, become incapacitated, or are no longer here, a third path opens after a long failsafe of inactivity — usually six to twelve months. Recovery requires two of your three keyholders, neither of whom is the platform. The long delay gives legitimate parties time to coordinate and gives any wrongful attempt time to be noticed. The structural independence of the keyholders — who, by design, are not related to one another — makes wrongful coordination logistically hard.

Each key lives on its holder’s own hardware signing device. The Sovereignty Backup Kit, generated at vault setup, lets you recover from your own vault using software we did not write. If Musonius is gone tomorrow, your vault still works. And if you simply want to leave while we are still here, you do not need our permission: wait the short timelock, use your inheritance keyholder, and move on. None of these mechanisms depend on us.

The honest part.

There are no perfect custody designs. Every architecture is a trade-off between sovereignty, convenience, recoverability, and resilience against attack. The honest engineers acknowledge this; the providers selling certainty do not.

Musonius cannot save you from every possible compound failure. If all three of your keys are lost at the same time, nothing survives. If both of your non-owner keyholders coordinate against you for the full failsafe period — without you noticing, without you able to act — they can recover the funds. The friction is long, the structural independence makes it hard, but the math does not strictly prevent it. We will not tell you it does.

What Musonius gives you is a vault where the math handles what math can handle, where the friction in the failure modes is built around independence and time, and where no party — including us — has the structural ability to collude with anyone.

We don't pretend otherwise. We just designed the trade-offs honestly.

Trust in math. Not in people.
